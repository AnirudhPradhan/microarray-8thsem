A B S T R A C T
The analysis of temporal data has gained increasing attention in recent years, aiming to identify patterns and
trends that change over time. Temporal triclustering is a promising approach for this purpose, as it allows for
the simultaneous clustering of three dimensions of data: objects, attributes, and time. In this work, we present a
comparative study and experimental evaluation of state-of-the-art temporal triclustering algorithms. Our study
provides a comprehensive quantitative assessment of several triclustering algorithms to unravel their strengths
and limitations. To this end, we consider synthetic data with varying sizes and regularities, where true solutions
are planted with different coherence and quality criteria, in order to assess the algorithms’ performance in
datasets with diverse characteristics and assess their capacity to retrieve specific types of hidden patterns. This
provides a more comprehensive evaluation of the algorithms and allows for a better understanding of their
capabilities and limitations. This study is the first to compare state-of-the-art triclustering algorithms inherently
prepared to deal with temporal data and provides new benchmark results for the Temporal Triclustering task.
Our results on the algorithms’ performance can guide practitioners in selecting the most appropriate algorithm
for their specific application.
1. Introduction
Three-way data (also referred to as three-dimensional, triadic, ten-
sor, or cubic data), more concretely temporal three-way data, have an
increasing prevalence in different fields of study as they offer a means
to capture the behavior of systems over time [ 1]. In this context, in
recent years, there has been a growing interest in using machine learn-
ing to analyze temporal data. Temporal data refers to data collected
over time and can be found in various applications, such as finance,
healthcare, and social media [1,2]. A critical task in analyzing temporal
data is to identify patterns and trends that change over time. One way
to do this is through the use of triclustering algorithms, which allow
for the simultaneous clustering of three dimensions of data: objects,
attributes, and time points.
In this work, we present a comparative study and experimental
evaluation of triclustering algorithms inherently prepared to process
temporal data, here referred as temporal triclustering algorithms. We
first provide an overview of the existing literature on triclustering
and the challenges that arise when applying it to temporal data. We
then thoroughly compare several state-of-the-art temporal triclustering
algorithms, pinpointing their strengths and limitations. Our study aims
to provide insights into the performance of these algorithms and to

2. Problem formulation
Three-way data. Three-way data (tridiac, three-dimensional, tensor, or
cubic data) is defined as a dataset with 𝑛 objects 𝑋 = { 𝑥1, … , 𝑥𝑛}, 𝑚
attributes 𝑌 = { 𝑦1, … , 𝑦𝑚} and 𝑝 contexts 𝑍 = { 𝑧1, … , 𝑧𝑝}. Elements
𝑎𝑖𝑗𝑘 relate object 𝑥𝑖, attribute 𝑦𝑗, and context 𝑧𝑘. Three-way data can
be real-valued ( 𝑎𝑖𝑗𝑘 ∈ R), symbolic ( 𝑎𝑖𝑗𝑘 ∈ 𝛴, where 𝛴 corresponds to
a set of ordinal or nominal symbols), or integer ( 𝑎𝑖𝑗𝑘 ∈ N) [1].
When the contexts correspond to time points, we encounter a tem-
poral three-way dataset, also known as a three-way time series. In this
type of dataset, each object represents a multivariate time series with
an order of 𝑚.
Tricluster. Given a 3W dataset 𝐻 with 𝑛 objects 𝑋, 𝑚 attributes 𝑌 , and
𝑝 contexts 𝑍, a tricluster 𝑡 = ( 𝐼, 𝐽 , 𝐾) is a subspace of the original
space, where 𝐼 ⊆ 𝑋 , 𝐽 ⊆ 𝑌 , and 𝐾 ⊆ 𝑍 are subsets of objects, features,
and contexts, respectively [1].
Triclustering solution. Given 𝐻, the triclustering task aims to find a set
of triclusters 𝑇 = {𝑡1, … , 𝑡𝑖} such that each tricluster 𝑡𝑖 satisfies specific
criteria of homogeneity and statistical significance [ 1].
Fig. 1 provides a visual representation of the concepts used in 3W
data analysis. In this figure, a tricluster is defined as subsets of objects
that exist simultaneously within each of the three dimensions.
3. Algorithms
During the last decade, many algorithms have been proposed to
address the challenges of finding subgroups concerning the three di-
mensions inherent in three-way data (objects, attributes, time). By
analyzing the literature, we can identify different categories of al-
gorithmic approaches. A survey on triclustering [ 1] categorizes the
algorithms into six groups (greedy, stochastic, exhaustive, biclustering-
based, pattern-based, multi-objective) that we refined here. Fig. 2
summarizes the five categories of algorithms we found in the literature
for addressing triclustering concerning temporal data. We refined three
of them into small subcategories, including algorithms with specific
similar behavior. The criteria for the categories and corresponding
subcategories’ definitions were the following:
1. Biclustering-based. Algorithms that perform biclustering (simi-
lar to triclustering but in two-way data) in a first phase, leading
to the discovery of triclusters using the biclusters identified in
slices.
(a) Biclusters Enumeration. Algorithms that enumerate all
the solutions (either biclusters or triclusters directly) for
the original problem. Typically these algorithms use a
graph search enumeration process.
(b) Similarity-based. These algorithms first search for bi-
clusters in slices and then merge these biclusters search-
ing for interplane coherence (similarities between planes)
to obtain triclusters.
2. Heuristic-search based. Algorithms that follow iterative
searches to find solutions.
(a) Divide-and-conquer. Algorithms that split the problem
into smaller problems and solve them individually, com-
bining the solutions to achieve the final solution to the
original problem.
(b) Greedy with seed growth. Algorithms that randomly
define a couple of seeds (small triclusters) and then con-
struct the solution iteratively regardless of the impact of
the decisions made at each iteration.
3. Pattern-based. Algorithms that perform pattern-based approa-
ches to find the triclustering solution.
(a) Association Rule Mining (ARM) based. These ap-
proaches use the concepts of frequent patterns to dis-
cover coherent patterns in three-way data, leading to the
triclusters composition.
(b) Formal Concept Analysis (FCA) based. FCA can be
used to discover triclusters in binary three-way data via
pattern extraction from a ternary relation between con-
cepts. Although none of the algorithms in literature had
been applied in temporal data, their potentialities can be
extended to deal with this type of data.
4. Stochastic. Algorithms that make assumptions about the statis-
tical distribution underlying the data trying to find triclusters
according to these statistical models.
5. Multi-objective Optimization. Algorithms that use merit func-
tions to optimize predefined objectives to be observed in triclus-
tering solution, usually, concerning volume and homogeneity.
Most of the algorithms in this category use evolutionary ap-
proaches, such as mutation, recombination and selection oper-
ations, to form triclusters.
In the following sections we briefly explain the ideas behind each
identified algorithm for each category.
3.1. Biclustering-based
Temporal three-way data space can be seen as a set of two-way
datasets (or simply matrices) corresponding to time points. Thus, bi-
clustering can be applied to discover sets of biclusters for each slice
of 3W data (temporal slice) from which a set of triclusters can be
deduced (according to specific interplane coherence). The initial strate-
gies considered here can be based on biclusters/triclusters enumeration.

These strategies entail exhaustively enumerating all potential solutions
for a given problem, either by initially focusing on biclusters and
subsequently inferring triclusters or by directly enumerating triclusters.
On the other hand, the subsequent approaches considered are based
on discovering biclusters for the slices of a given dimension and then
merging the biclusters using a similarity-based metric to obtain the
triclusters.
We start by identifying four algorithms that perform biclustering
enumeration. triCluster [3] was the pioneer triclustering algorithm
and was designed to deal with gene expression data. It first con-
structs a multigraph with similar ranges between all pairs of objects
(genes)for each temporal slice in 3W data, following it searches for
maximal cliques in the constructed multigraphs to find the sets of
biclusters per time point; finally, it merges the similar biclusters from
the search in a new multigraph where the nodes are now biclusters.
gTriCluster [4] includes intertemporal coherence, which was a gap in
the first considered algorithm, using Spearman correlation coefficient
between time points. In terms of behavior, this algorithm is similar to
the previous one, improving by using dedicated data structures (set
enumeration trees) to mine the biclusters. TD-Clustering [5] aims
to find time-delayed scaling, shifting or inverting triclusters in 3W
temporal data. This algorithm performs a depth-first search through
a tree structure to mine all maximal biclusters satisfying a minimum
number of objects and time points according to a predefined value of
coherence strength. TCtriCluster [2,6] is an extension of the original
triCluster algorithm able to find time contiguous triclusters by imposing
a coherence constraint of contiguity when merging biclusters from the
multigraph, thus searching only for biclusters contiguous in time to be
merged in the resulted triclusters.
The following four algorithms use a similarity-based strategy to
merge biclusters into triclusters. ICSM [7] performs order-preserving
biclustering in each context slice and merges the biclusters according
to time dimension using a Planar Mean Residue Similarity (PMRS).
OPTricluster [8] finds triclusters with order-preserving coherence in
the attribute slices, while removing contiguity in the time dimen-
sion the placed coherence ensures less sensitivity to variations. THD-
Tricluster [9] identifies triclusters over gene-sample-time data with
any absolute, shifting, scaling and shifting-and-scaling patterns, and
high biological significance. It introduces a coherence measure (using
Shifting-and-Scaling Similarity - SSSim) referred to as Inter-temporal
to support effective clustering for the temporal domain. POPTric [10]
uses a pathway-based approach to identify patterns in gene expression
data while also preserving the order of genes, samples, and time points
to identify subsets of genes that are co-regulated over time in specific
biological pathways.
3.2. Heuristic-search based
Divide-and-conquer algorithms split the problem into smaller prob-
lems and solve each one individually to find the solution for the initial
large problem. We considered two algorithms following this approach.
TRI-Clustering [11] uses an Automatic Boundary Search (ABS) to
unravel coherent triclusters with differential coherence in object slices.
It starts by generating random seeds and trims triclusters until the
convergence (maximal iterations reached). 𝛿-TRIMAX [12–14] finds
triclusters with low mean square residue (MSR) in temporal 3W data.
Initially, 𝛿-TRIMAX considers the whole 3W dataset (objects, attributes,
and contexts) as a candidate tricluster. At each iteration, some objects
are deleted until the solution’s MSR becomes lower than a predefined
threshold (MSR < 𝛿 ). Then, new objects are added to the solution if
they do not cause the MSR to exceed the 𝛿 threshold.
On the other hand, greedy approaches plant seeds (building blocks),
instead of considering the whole 3W dataset at as a starting point. They
start with small solutions that are gradually extended to form candidate
triclusters. MIC [15] and LagMiner [16] are two examples of this type
of approach. MIC aims to find statistically significant triclusters in
temporal 3W data with an approximate constant coherence. To this
end, MIC first generates seeds (pairs of correlated objects) with residual
p-values. Then it grows correlated 3D subspace clusters (CSCs) from
these seeds by greedily extending and merging seeds that maximize the
proposed correlation scores. On its turn, with the same basis,LagMiner
aims to find shift-and-scale correlated triclusters ( 𝑆2𝐷3-Clusters) in
gene-sample-time expression data. The found triclusters have to respect
two predefined criteria: (1) shifting-and-scaling intraplane coherence
for each time point (gene-sample slice) and object (time-gene slice),
and (2) an order-preserving intraplane coherence within each sample
(gene-time slice).
3.3. Pattern-based approaches
Specific, well-defined patterns in 3W subspaces can be mined using
pattern-based triclustering approaches based on traditional association
rule mining (ARM). We found five algorithms following this strategy.
Ji et al. [17] propose a naive approach, referred to as RSM, that
explores the frequent close patterns in data slices, merging them into
frequent close cubes (FCC), and pruning the ones that are not closed.
In the same work [17], CubeMiner directly operates on 3W data to
discover FCCs splitting the data according to well-defined constraints.
3D-TDAR-Mine [18] identifies triclusters given by Temporal Depen-
dency Association Rules (TDAR) in temporal data. It does not require
discretization, and the placed coherence is able to accommodate shifts,
scales, and trends. It starts by computing patterns per object, where
coherent time segments represent patterns for at least two attributes.
Frequent pattern mining is applied to find the common patterns in
multiple objects, and then using association rule mining is used to dis-
cover dependencies between patterns to compose triclusters. Versatile
Temporal Subspace discovery [19] describes a tricluster by a clear
temporal pattern given by the longest possible smooth sections of non-
overlapping waveforms using the LOESS residue (computed from the
lowest-order polynomial fitting the observed values). An exhaustive
prefix-based search algorithm is then proposed with Pearson correlation
(parameterized with the LOESS residue) to discover all maximal triclus-
ters from closed temporal patterns. Due to the potential high number of
triclusters, the top-k triclusters can be alternatively outputted. Mondal
et al. [20] introduced suffix trees to search for triclusters [20]. The
algorithm uses a suffix tree data structure to efficiently search for

Another strategy included in this category is regarding Formal
Concept Analysis . All the proposed algorithms based on Formal Con-
cept Analysis deal with binary data, where each entry represents a
relation between three concepts, termed triadic formal concepts. The
main principle of this type of algorithm is to search all the possible
pairs of objects and attributes exhaustively and then assemble them
in tricluters. They use quality scores based on density, cardinality,
coverage and diversity of subspaces to guide the search for coherent
triclusters. Many algorithms (exploring several approaches) were de-
signed in the last years [21–23], after the concept formalization made
by Ignatov et al. [24]. Although none of the algorithms is able to handle
temporal or real-valued data inherently, based on the work in [25],
the underlying principles show sufficient evidence to be extended for
triclustering temporal 3W data.
3.4. Stochastic
Stochastic algorithms assume that data follows a known statisti-
cal distribution and try to find the model’s parameters that allow
generating of the data under analysis. We identified four algorithms
in this category. Gene Program [26] finds triclusters using Markov
Chain Monte Carlo sampling to estimate a model’s posterior probability
distribution of subspaces after applying a discretization method to
the 3D real-valued dataset. MultiFacTV [27] uses a tensor factoriza-
tion objective function (holding an approximate constant coherence
assumption with plaid effects), together with a time-axis regularization
(enforcing subspaces to have objects as contiguous as possible in the
time dimension). Triclusters are then derived from the factorized ten-
sors. This algorithm requires the specification of the target number of
triclusters and thresholding parameters per dimension. A triclustering
plaid model, referred here as 3D-Plaid, was introduced in [28], where
the whole 3W data structure is seen as a set of overlapping triclusters
from where the algorithm tries to iteratively find subspaces expressing
high deviations verifying their dependence through minimizing the
sum of squares of the 3W data. TWIGS [29] tries to find triclusters
in 3D temporal data, where objects may be temporally misaligned.
A hierarchical Bayesian model is used to find biclusters across object
slices to then produce candidate triclusters. Gibbs sampling is applied
to optimize the model’s parameters.
3.5. Multiobjective
The triclustering task can be seen as a multi-objective optimization
problem since we typically have different objectives clashing with
each other, such as volume and homogeneity. It is a trade-off be-
cause the increased volume will lead to reduced homogeneity. All
the algorithms included here have evolutionary approaches, having
in their basis operations such as exchanges, mutations, and selections
of objects. MOGA3C [30], TriGen [31] and EMOA-𝛿-TRIMAX [32]
fall in this category. The pioneer algorithm following this approach
was MOGA3C [30]. This algorithm aims to satisfy three simultaneous
objectives: volume, homogeneity and object(gene)-dimension variance.
TriGen [31], in its first version, is guided by two objectives: maximize
triclusters’ volume and minimize 3D mean square residue. TriGen was
recently improved to yield a flexible form of coherence with two
new merit functions: MSL [33], and LSL [34]. These new measures
enable the possibility to accommodate time lags on object dimensions.
EMOA-𝛿-TRIMAX [32] introduced a multiobjective search improving
𝛿-TRIMAX [12] preventing overlapping while searching to maximize
volume and minimize MSR 3D.
4. Triclustering evaluation
In this study, we assess state-of-the-art triclustering algorithms from
the aforementioned categories: Biclustering-based, Heuristic-search
based, and Multi-objective optimization. Current pattern-based ap-
proaches are not designed to address real temporal data properly, and
the stochastic approaches force the definition of a strict number of
patterns which can bias the evaluation. To this end, we evaluate each
algorithm’s solution against true (hidden) solutions using synthetic
datasets with planted triclusters by G-Tric [35], a software tool to
generate three-way datasets with hidden true triclustering solutions.
An objective evaluation in real datasets would not be possible since no
true solutions are available.
Horta and Campello introduced a set of desirable properties for the
metric used to evaluate biclustering solutions [36]. Building upon their
work, we now propose an extension of these criteria that evaluation
metrics should satisfy in order to provide a comprehensive assessment
of triclustering solutions:
• Maximum: an equivalent solution should have a maximum score;
• Symmetry: the order in which the subspace clusters are repre-
sented should not influence the outcome;
• Dissimilarity: penalizes solutions with redundant triclusters;
• Precision: penalizes solutions that use more than one subspace to
cover a single tricluster;
• Non-intersecting Area: penalizes quality decays when a tricluster
includes other tricluster’s elements;
• Coverage: a solution is supposed to cover all planted subspaces;
and
• Statistical Significance: ensures maximal solutions without spurious
subspaces.

5. Methods
This section introduces the methodology followed to perform the
comprehensive assessment and comparison of the state-of-the-art tri-
clustering algorithms inherently prepared to deal with temporal data.
5.1. Algorithms
For this experimental evaluation, we considered triclustering algo-
rithms from three of the categories defined before: Biclustering-based,
Heuristic-search based, and Multi-objective optimization. We selected
four algorithms for this study: triCluster, TCtriCluster, 𝛿-trimax, and
TriGen. These are those in the literature explicitly designed to tackle
temporal 3W data analysis, whose implementations are available. Ta-
ble 1 lists the selected algorithms and the links to the public source
code. The majority of the triclustering algorithms referred to in the
literature have no implementations available. We attempted to reach
out to the authors to obtain source codes from other algorithms but
with limited success.
5.2. Data collections
Evaluating triclustering algorithms is challenging due to the absence
of public datasets with known triclustering solutions, hindering the
comparison of different algorithms and the assessment of each ap-
proach’s strengths and weaknesses. Lobo et al. proposed G-Tric [35]
to address this problem by presenting a tool for generating synthetic
three-way datasets with triclustering solutions. This was the first pro-
posal to tackle a key lack for triclustering evaluation and remains the
state-of-the-art.
This three-way data generator allows the generation of datasets
for different scenarios, including different types of triclustering pat-
terns, pattern-specific datasets, datasets with overlapping triclusters,
and datasets with noise and error values. These datasets enable to
understand how the algorithms perform under different conditions and
to identify issues and direction for improvement. G-Tric can also be
used to generate synthetic datasets for real-world applications where
the triclustering solutions are unknown, providing a way to evaluate
the algorithm’s results in a controlled setting. In this work, G-Tric is
used to generate several datasets with hidden solutions to evaluate the
selected triclustering algorithms, using the settings described in the
following section, which align with the proposed in [35].
Synthetic data were generated with varying characteristics to simu-
late different scenarios in five evaluation experiments with the follow-
ing settings:
1. The first experiment uses a real-valued baseline dataset with
500 rows, 10 columns, and 5 contexts, containing 40 triclus-
ters evenly distributed among four types of triclustering pat-
terns: order preserving on contexts, fully constant, fully addi-
tive, and fully multiplicative. The planted triclusters covered
approximately 17% of the dataset.
2. The second experiment uses four datasets with the same features,
each containing patterns of only one type. The patterns were
applied to each combination of dimensions ( 𝑋, 𝑌 , 𝑍), specified
in the first three columns of Tables 3 and 4. The Order Preserving
(OP) dataset had 30 triclusters (the maximum number of this

5.3. Evaluation of results
The performance of the triclustering algorithms was evaluated using
the three introduced metrics: Recoverability (R), Clustering Error (CE),
and Revised Match Score 3D (RMS3).
Recoverability measures the proportion of planted triclusters that
were correctly detected by the algorithm. A higher recoverability score
indicates that the algorithm is better at detecting planted triclusters.
CE measures the dissimilarity between the detected triclusters and the
planted ones. A lower clustering error indicates that the detected tri-
clusters are more similar to the planted ones. RMS3, on the other hand,
evaluates the similarity in detail to all the triclustering dimensions
and penalizes the non-matched elements. A poor similarity in one of
the dimensions can negatively influence the metric value. The results
for each algorithm were obtained by running the algorithms with
the generated datasets. The results were then compared and analyzed
to determine which algorithm performed best in terms of the three
metrics. Specific assessments about the types of planted patterns were
also performed to get more insights into the algorithm’s behavior.
6. Results and discussion
6.1. Experiments with the first data collection (baseline)
The results of applying the selected triclustering algorithms to the
first collection of datasets are presented in Fig. 3. TriGen (with MSL
merit function) performed the best in terms of recovering the planted
triclusters. The results of TriGen are in line with those obtained by
the authors when comparing the three merit functions, with LSL per-
forming better than the others [38]. However, TriCluster achieved the
second-best result in terms of recoverability, yielding a lower Clustering
Error. This indicates that the method may be capable of finding a
subset of the planted subspaces but not the entire set. This highlights
the importance of considering the varying coherence types present
in the dataset since each algorithm specializes in only some types.
Therefore, it is not advisable to make premature conclusions about
accuracy without further analysis.
To gain a deeper understanding of each algorithm’s behavior, the
use of ground truth data and extrinsic metrics enables an analysis of
the impact of specific patterns on the performance of the algorithms.
Fig. 4(A), (B), and (C) summarize the R, CE, and RMS3 scores of
each algorithm based on subspaces with a single type of coherence.
This allows for a more informed assessment of the success of each
method in finding the target triclusters and differentiating between
expected and unexpected results. As TriCluster is capable of detecting
shifting and scaling patterns, it is expected to have good performance
when retrieving Constant, Additive, and Multiplicative patterns. The
method indeed achieved perfect scores on Constant and Multiplicative
patterns, but failed to find the Additive ones. Further investigation
may be necessary to understand why this is the case and whether the
dimension in which coherence is applied yields significant impact on
the results. Moreover, we could verify that TriGen was able to recover
a higher number of the planted patterns, but, given the maximization
of triclusters as one of the objectives, its performance can be degraded
by a high clustering error.
6.2. Experiments with the second data collection
To further examine the performance of the algorithms, a second set
of datasets were evaluated, with results summarized in Tables 3 and 4.
These results indicate generalized difficulties by 𝛿-trimax and Tri-
Gen (MSR3D) in finding the planted triclusters with different types
of coherence. TriCluster performs well in the majority of cases where
the pattern is additive but struggles with fully additive or additive
patterns in both the object and attribute dimensions. This could be due
to the biclustering approach used in the method, which only detects
shifting patterns in one dimension. Conversely, TriGen (using LSL and
MSL merit functions) performs well in detecting constant patterns
and has the best results with fully additive patterns. TriGen, given
its multiobjective behavior, arguably appears to be the most versatile
algorithm able to recover patterns from all the planted types. However,
its higher clustering errors reveal its difficulty in controlling precision
(extraction of non-planted data elements).
Analyzing the matching quality per dimension provides insights into
how each algorithm deals with different patterns and what dimensions
they search for coherence. This type of analysis also pinpoints any
limitations in the methods, which may not be immediately evident
without using synthetic data with ground truth.
In summary, the results show that TriCluster and TriGen (MSL and
MSR3D) perform well in terms of recoverability, while TriGen (MSL and
MSR3D) have low clustering error and RMS3 scores. TCtriCluster and𝛿-
Trimax show lower performance across all three metrics. In particular,
for the Order Preserving pattern type, TriGen (MSL, MSR3D, and LSL)

6.3. Experiments with the third data collection
The third experiment in this comparison examines the effect of
overlapping on the performance of each algorithm. The datasets used in
this experiment were derived from the baseline dataset and modified to
have the overlapping properties outlined in Table 2. The key differences
from the baseline data include: 40% of the planted triclusters overlap
with another subspace in a pairwise manner, meaning there are no
sets of three or more triclusters overlapping. Each pair of overlapping
triclusters shares a maximum of 50% of their elements relative to the
smallest tricluster, and there is no limit on the amount of overlap
permitted along each dimension. Fig. 4(D), (E), and (F) resume the
performance results of the triclustering algorithms on each of the four
datasets, one per pattern type, with overlapping. The results remained
similar regarding all the assessed metrics, with Order Preserving set-
tings continuing with poor performance. This is somewhat expected
since none of the evaluated algorithms was designed to identify this
type of pattern. However, TriGen (MSL) achieved high Recoverability
with a small decrease compared with the baseline (0.75 vs. 0.82).
Nonetheless, the results are promising when investigating algorith-
mic behaviors with other patterns. On the Multiplicative dataset, the
CE score was not affected in the majority of the algorithms, having
decreased only for TCtriCluster (0.82 vs. 0.79). Regarding the other
patterns, this score was not affected.
Recoverability, in general, was not also affected. TriGen (LSL) in-
creased the R score (0.73 vs. 0.74), looking for Additive patterns. This
might be a consequence of the plaid coherency assumed (Additive),
which, as opposed to what happened with the other patterns, does not
make the overlapped region follow the same coherency type of the
original triclusters.
In short, TriCluster, in fact, exhibits difficulties handling overlap-
ping regions, as pinpointed by the authors [3]. Something highlighted
as difficult that would be difficult to evaluate in the absence of data’s
ground truth.
6.4. Experiments with the fourth data collection
A crucial aspect impacting an algorithm’s performance is the quality
of the data it is tested on. Poor data quality makes it harder to identify
coherent subspaces that can form triclusters. By using synthetic data
with ground truth for analysis, we can determine which algorithms are
better suited to deal with varying forms and degrees of noise, or at least
can minimize its effect. The final group of datasets was based on the
baseline dataset but with a degree of added noise and gross errors both
in the background and individual triclusters.
The results are depicted in Fig. 5(G), (H), and (I) show that the
ongoing underperformance of 𝛿-trimax in recovering triclusters in the
Additive and Multiplicative datasets can be attributed to the search for
patterns with an MSR3D below a specific delta. This approach may lead
to a high number of false positives due to the lack of statistical signifi-
cance guarantees. Additionally, some false negatives may occur because
of how the MSR combines contributions from different dimensions.
All algorithms, except TriGen, saw a decline in performance due
to the added noise. TriGen remained relatively stable in terms of
recoverability but also showed high clustering error, meaning that the
triclusters found were larger than expected, as indicated by lower RMS3
values. Results on the algorithms’ recoverability measure also showed
poor performance, with a few small improvements in some metrics
driven by the recovery of larger triclusters or solutions rather than the
algorithms’ ability to retrieve the subspaces accurately. None of the
algorithms was able to effectively deal with varying forms and degrees
of noise, or minimize its effect.
These observations are not surprising, as none of the evaluated
algorithms were explicitly designed to tolerate varying degrees of noise
in spite of their ability to accommodate it. However, TriGen was found
to be the only algorithm that remained unaffected due to its multi-
objective nature with fitness functions that evaluate the interactions
between objects. The presence of noisy elements within the data poses
a significant challenge to the discovery of relevant triclusters as real-
world patterns are generally affected by noise. Therefore, it is crucial to
tackle this limitation in future research to enable the effective discovery
of such triclusters.
6.5. Experiments with the fifth data collection
The temporal aspect requires the application of specific constraints
to the subspaces being investigated. In particular, the contiguity of
tricluster’s time points when focusing on subsets of objects, features,
and times to ensure the stability of coherence along time. The latest
data collection followed the same properties as the previous one, but
this time, we only focused on triclusters with contiguity in the temporal
dimension.
In this latest experiment, TriGen performed well in terms of recov-
erability, as shown in Fig. 5(J), (K), and (L). In particular, TriCluster
and TCtricluster, which showed similar recoverability (with TCtri-
cluster having a slight improvement), are competitive with TriGen’s
performance in the Multiplicative dataset. TCtriCluster was specifically
designed to find temporally contiguous triclusters [2], and, as the
results show, it remains the best option for achieving this goal by
obtaining the lowest clustering error and the highest RMS3.
The ability to find contiguity in temporal patterns while preserving
specific coherence, although particularly addressed by the TCtriCluster

7. Conclusion and future research directions
The emergence of triclustering algorithms and their notable applica-
tions has triggered the need for their solid evaluation and comparison
as a way of guiding the selection of the most effective approaches for
a given data context and specific application. This study provides a
significant contribution by establishing a benchmark for assessing tem-
poral triclustering algorithms, allowing for fairer comparisons in the
future. We performed a comprehensive assessment of four algorithms
using five synthetic data collections with planted triclusters (ground
truth), evaluating the capabilities of the algorithms to find triclusters
with different types of pattern coherence in 3W datasets with varying
characteristics. The results pinpointed the strengths and limitations of
the assessed algorithms in three-way temporal data analysis.

This study represents a noteworthy advance in the triclustering
research field as it provides a clear baseline for future developments
to build upon. Future research directions based on the limitations of
the assessed algorithms include: (1) addressing temporal misalignments
through enhanced methods; (2) investigating triclustering techniques
designed explicitly for sparse 3D data; (3) preparing triclustering meth-
